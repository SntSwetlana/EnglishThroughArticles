# Wildlife Returns to Chernobyl

Forty years after the Chernobyl nuclear disaster, the exclusion zone around the former power plant has become one of Europe’s most unusual wildlife sanctuaries. Although the accident initially caused severe damage to local ecosystems, the long-term absence of human activity has allowed many species to return and, in some cases, flourish.

The Chernobyl Exclusion Zone was established after the 1986 accident, which released radioactive material across large areas of Europe and forced the evacuation of nearby settlements. The zone now covers thousands of square kilometres where permanent human habitation, commercial activity, public access and natural resource extraction are severely restricted. As a result, the area has become an unintended experiment in large-scale rewilding.

Large mammals have increased significantly in parts of the zone. Wolves, foxes, Eurasian lynx, elk and wild boar have become more common, while species such as brown bears and European bison have also returned. Some studies suggest that the absence of hunting, farming and urban development has benefited animal populations more than radiation has harmed them.

This does not mean that radiation has been harmless. In the early years after the accident, plants and animals suffered serious damage, especially in the Red Forest, a highly contaminated area near the reactor where pine trees absorbed large amounts of radiation and turned reddish-brown before dying. However, long-term research indicates that biodiversity has increased in many parts of the exclusion zone since humans left.

Several rare species have also returned. Przewalski’s horses, introduced in the Ukrainian part of the zone in 1998 as a conservation experiment, now form a thriving population. Eurasian lynx and European bison have re-established themselves, and bird species such as black storks, white storks and white-tailed eagles have been recorded in the area.

One of the most important examples is the greater spotted eagle, a globally endangered bird that depends on wetland habitats and is highly sensitive to human disturbance. The species had disappeared from the region at the time of the accident, but breeding pairs have since been documented in the Belarusian part of the zone. Today, this region is considered one of the few places where its population is increasing.

Scientists have also found evidence that some organisms may be adapting to radioactive conditions. Tree frogs in the area are darker than usual, possibly because higher melanin levels help protect them against radiation damage. Research on wolves suggests that some populations may be developing resilience to chronic radiation exposure, although the full implications remain uncertain.

Adaptation is not limited to animals. Black fungi discovered inside the damaged reactor appear to use melanin in ways that may help them tolerate or even exploit radiation. Some plants in contaminated areas also show DNA repair responses and enhanced tolerance of radiation and heavy metals. These findings make the exclusion zone an important site for studying ecosystem recovery under extreme conditions.

Nevertheless, the ecological story of Chernobyl is not simply positive. Some species show reduced reproductive success, higher mutation rates and health problems linked to radiation exposure. The zone has been shaped by radiation, abandonment and time, producing outcomes that are uneven and difficult to predict.

Similar patterns have been observed around other nuclear exclusion zones, including Fukushima, where mammals such as bears, raccoons and wild boars have increased in areas with limited human presence. These cases suggest that human disturbance can sometimes be more damaging to wildlife than contaminated environments, although this conclusion should not be used to minimise the harm caused by nuclear disasters.

Chernobyl therefore offers no simple lesson. It should not take a catastrophe to protect ecosystems from human pressure. Yet the return of wildlife to the exclusion zone shows that nature can respond powerfully when human activity is removed. The area has become both a warning about environmental disaster and a reminder of ecological resilience.
